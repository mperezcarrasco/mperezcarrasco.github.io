@ Automatic Learning for the Rapid Classification of Events (ALeRCE)

Anomaly Detection Algorithm for Astronomical Light Curves

ALeRCE is a Chilean-led astro- nomical broker which uses a Cloud Infrastructure and Machine Learning for processing the alert stream from the Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF) survey and which aims to become a Community Broker for the Vera C. Rubin Observatory and its Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) (which will be the biggest telescope in the world!). In ALeRCE I am responsible for implementing a novelty detection algorithm using the astro- nomical light curves coming from the data stream. The main goal is to detect objects that do not belong to our taxonomy and send alerts for the astronomers to perform astrophysics and make interesting discoveries. Our objective is to be the firsts in detecting new astronomical objects, thereby contributing to a better understanding of where we are living and what is around us. ALeRCE’s presentation paper submitted to American Astronomical Society Journals. Repository

@ Biomedical Laboratory, UdeC

Real-time EEG-based Drowsiness Detection Algorithm

Drowsy driving is a major problem in road safety. This issue is responsible for a huge number of crashes, injuries, and deaths along the world, and no one is exempt from this. At the Biomedical Laboratory of UdeC I am responsible for implementing a machine learning system for drowsiness detection using EEG signals extracted from a 16-channels g.Nautilus EEG headset. There are a lot of challenges for this task, such as the need for reduced amount of channels coming from the signal for this system be ergonomic, the high cost of the signal labeling process, and the need for real-time detection. For that reason, we are implementing a transfer learning algorithm that learns from vast annotated public sources and uses that knowledge to makes our low annotated signals perform better. Thereby reducing the number of channels, while it does not increment the labeling efforts. Also, the system is running almost in real-time! Repository will be released soon.

@ UdeC/Harvard U.

Semi-supervised Domain Adaptation for Image Classification Through Generative Models

Deep learning models have demonstrated to be very good at discovering underlying patterns behind the data. But for these models works correctly it is usually needed a large amount of annotated data in order to avoid the overfitting and be able to generalize over new data. Unfortunately, in most of real-life cases, labeling data is costly and time-consuming. Domain adaptation models aim at generalizing a model trained in a vast annotated source to perform well in a low annotated target by finding a common-shared representation between them. I started this project as my master thesis, being able to tackle it through semi-supervised learning and generative models, becoming the state-of-the-art in low-dimensional benchmarks (please refer to my Astroinformatics2019’s talk for more information). At this moment I am working on extending the model to more complex high dimensional data. The final goal is to create a robust domain adaptation model that uses labeled and unlabeled data and generative models to tackle the overfitting produced when models are trained using the extremely low amount of labeled data. Paper in preparation. Previous results can be found here. Repository will be released soon.

@ UdeC

Attributes Transfer in Deep Neural Network Aplied to Astronomical Images

The galaxy evolution through time is one of the key questions of modern astrophysics and the study of their morphologies represents a fundamental and powerful diagnosis to evolutionary changes of galaxies. Due to the increase in astronomical data volume, for my undergrad thesis I created a new catalog of galaxy morphologies for “Cluster Lensing and Supernova survey with Hubble (CLASH)” using deep neural networks. As was mentioned before, annotated data is in most of real-life cases costly and time- consuming, while sometimes there are similar or related other sources available and we can take advantage of them to make our few labeled target data performs better. For this project, we took advantage of a vast labeled source named “The Cosmic Assembly Near-infrared Deep Extragalactic Legacy Survey (CANDELS)” consisting of images associated with 5 different galaxy morphologies, labeled by expert astronomers using visual inspection. We used the same taxonomy and created a few eyeball labels. As in some cases there exist disagreement between expert opinions, this was a regression problem. We used different deep learning architectures and a transfer learning technique named fine-tuning and we showed the effectiveness of this method. Finally, we released a public Catalog for 8,412 galaxies of CLASH. Paper published in Publications of the Astronomical Society of the Pacific, 2019. Repository.
